\section{Introduction}

Robot foundation models---VLA policies and imitation-learning architectures---have made major advances in robotic capability~\citep{openvla_paper, act_paper}. Yet despite these gains, reliable generalization and safe deployment in open-world environments remain difficult due to distribution shifts, ambiguous states, and the unpredictability of real-world interaction.

Recent work has shown that policy hidden states encode failure signals: SAFE~\citep{safe_paper} trains classifiers on VLA features for binary failure detection. We take it a step further asking if these representations encode not just \emph{whether} failure will occur, but \emph{when} and \emph{how to correct}? If so, we can move beyond detection-and-stop to continuous inference-time correction.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
    \item[\textbf{C1}] \textbf{From detection to correction.} We extend failure-detection probes to output continuous Cartesian corrections and time-to-failure estimates across OpenVLA (4096-d) and ACT (256-d).
    \item[\textbf{C2}] \textbf{Detection-gated perturbation as mechanism.} We attempted continuous steering but found that the learned correction direction does not outperform random perturbation (jiggle ablation). The failure-detection timing---not the correction vector---is the operative safety component. This simplifies the design space and identifies correction fidelity as the key bottleneck for future work.
    \item[\textbf{C3}] \textbf{Cross-architecture generalization at low latency.} The probe self-calibrates to task difficulty ($r{=}{-}0.98$, $p{<}10^{-6}$; $n{=}10$ tasks), generalizes zero-shot, and matches MPPI at $6{\times}$ lower latency across a $16{\times}$ dimensionality gap.
\end{itemize}
