\subsection{Mechanism Analysis}
\label{sec:mechanism}

The jiggle baseline uses random correction direction at matched magnitude. \textbf{Result:} no significant difference from steering. The failure-detection head---not the correction direction---is the operative mechanism. Safety adapters should prioritize detection (AUC 0.83--0.89) over correction direction.

Under $2{-}5{\times}$ baseline disturbance on the four hardest tasks (Table~\ref{tab:stronger_ood}, Figure~\ref{fig:ood_push_sweep}), steering shows $+3.7$\,pp at $3{\times}$ push ($p{=}0.11$) but the pooled effect ($+0.6$\,pp, $p{=}0.42$) is not significant. Jiggle degrades more than steering at the strongest levels, consistent with the mechanism analysis.

\subsection{Cross-Model Transfer}
\label{sec:transfer}

We test whether a safety head trained on one architecture detects failure on another via learned linear projection. Linear transfer fails: ACT$\to$OpenVLA AUC = 0.27 (native 0.70); OpenVLA$\to$ACT = 0.42 (native 0.94). The safety signal exists in both architectures but the coordinate systems are not linearly alignable (Figure~\ref{fig:transfer} in Appendix). Nonlinear transfer is future work.
