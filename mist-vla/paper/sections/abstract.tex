\begin{abstract}
Recent work shows that robot policy hidden states encode failure signals detectable by lightweight classifiers. We ask: do they also encode \emph{when} failure will occur and \emph{how to correct}?

We present \textbf{PULSE} (\textbf{P}robing \textbf{U}nderlying \textbf{L}atent \textbf{S}afety \textbf{E}mbeddings), which extends failure detection to continuous spatial correction. An MLP probe achieves failure-prediction AUC 0.83--0.98 and time-to-failure correlation $r{=}0.86$ across both a 7B-parameter VLA (OpenVLA, 4096-d) and a compact imitation learner (ACT, 256-d).

PULSE reads these signals to apply bounded corrective steering at inference time, without retraining the base policy. The probe self-calibrates to task difficulty ($r{=}{-}0.98$, $p{<}10^{-6}$), generalizes zero-shot to held-out tasks, and matches sampling-based planning (MPPI) at $6{\times}$ lower latency. A mechanism analysis reveals that detection-gated perturbation---not learned correction direction---is the operative component: any bounded perturbation timed by the failure signal prevents catastrophic trajectory commitment. This simplifies the safety-adapter design space and identifies correction fidelity as the key bottleneck for future work.

\end{abstract}
