\begin{abstract}
Recent work shows that robot policy hidden states encode failure signals detectable by lightweight classifiers. We ask: do they also encode \emph{when} failure will occur and \emph{how to correct}?

We present \textbf{PULSE} (Probing Underlying Latent Safety Embeddings), which extends failure detection to include temporal prediction and correction estimation. An MLP probe achieves failure-prediction AUC 0.83--0.98 and time-to-failure correlation $r{=}0.86$ across both a 7B-parameter VLA (OpenVLA, 4096-d) and a compact imitation learner (ACT, 256-d).

We attempted to use these signals for bounded corrective steering at inference time. The probe self-calibrates to task difficulty ($r{=}{-}0.98$, $p{<}10^{-6}$), generalizes zero-shot, and matches MPPI at $6{\times}$ lower latency. However, a mechanism analysis reveals that the learned correction direction does not outperform random perturbation---the failure-detection timing, not the correction vector, is what matters. This identifies correction fidelity as the key bottleneck and simplifies the safety-adapter design space: knowing \emph{when} to intervene is the hard problem, not \emph{where} to push.

\end{abstract}
