\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Detection is strong and cross-architectural.}
The probe achieves strong failure detection and TTF prediction across a $16{\times}$ dimensionality gap. This extends prior detection-only work by showing the signal is rich enough to extract temporal structure, not just binary failure labels.

\paragraph{The correction direction does not help.}
The jiggle ablation is the most important result in this paper, and it is a negative one: matched-magnitude random corrections perform comparably to our learned corrections. In plain terms, the MLP learns \emph{when} the robot is in trouble but not \emph{which direction} to push it. A random nudge timed by the failure signal works just as well as the learned correction vector. This does not mean the system is useless---the detection timing is genuinely valuable (AUC 0.83--0.98)---but the correction head needs fundamental improvement before steering can outperform vanilla execution. We attempted continuous spatial correction and it did not succeed; improving correction fidelity is the primary direction for future work.

\paragraph{Adaptive gating is emergent.}
The $r{=}{-}0.98$ correlation ($p{<}10^{-6}$) between intervention rate and difficulty arises without explicit difficulty signal. The double-gating mechanism naturally modulates intervention density based on latent-state uncertainty.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Pooled success differences are not significant---steering does not improve over vanilla. Correction direction $\approx$ random. OOD effect is modest. Gating correlation is on $n{=}10$ tasks. All current results are in simulation; {\color{blue}physical robot validation on SO-101 arms is planned.}
